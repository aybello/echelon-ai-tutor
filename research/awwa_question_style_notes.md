# AWWA Wastewater Operator Study Guide — Question Style Audit Notes

Source: `Giorgi, John. Wastewater Operator Certification Study Guide: A Guide to Preparing for Wastewater Treatment Certification Exams` (AWWA, 2009).

## Initial findings from pages 1–5

- The guide is a formal certification-prep resource published by the American Water Works Association.
- It is explicitly organized by grade/class level, which suggests question difficulty is intentionally tiered by certification level.
- The contents pages show the exam material is structured by topic area rather than by long narrative scenarios.
- Topics are broken into concise operational domains such as treatment principles, facilities, screens/grit removal, sedimentation, activated sludge, disinfection, solids handling, maintenance, safety, and chemistry.
- The front matter includes a disclaimer noting that questions may sometimes be disputed factually or by interpretation, which implies the authors expect close distractor quality and realistic ambiguity rather than obvious throwaway options.

## Working hypotheses for question-writing style to verify in later pages

- Questions are likely concise and operationally framed.
- Answer choices are likely similar in length and plausibility.
- Difficulty likely increases through topic complexity and judgment required, not by making the correct answer conspicuously longer.
- Distractors are likely technically credible alternatives, not absurd decoys.
- Topic coverage likely mirrors actual operator responsibilities and certification scope.

## Additional findings from pages 8–12

The later front matter confirms the guide's stated purpose: it is intended to give operators practice answering questions that are similar in both format and content to certification exam questions. That wording matters because it indicates the book is not merely explanatory; it is deliberately imitating exam-style item writing. The preface also states that the study guide does not reproduce the exact certification exam questions, but instead prepares candidates through comparable structure and content. This supports using the guide as a style reference rather than a source to copy.

## Findings from pages 14–17: actual Grade 1 question pages

The first real question pages clarify the exam-writing style very strongly. The questions are short, direct, and operational rather than verbose. Most stems ask for a definition, typical value, basic cause-and-effect relationship, or standard operating fact. The options are compact and fairly similar in length. Even when one option is correct and the others are wrong, the distractors are not written as exaggerated throwaways; they remain within the same semantic category as the correct answer. For example, when the question asks about anaerobic conditions or wastewater solids, the distractors are other plausible technical terms, not absurd jokes or obviously irrelevant phrases.

A second important pattern is that the correct answer is not signaled by being longer or more detailed. In many cases all four options are just one word, one phrase, or one numerical range. This means the candidate must actually know the content, because test-taking heuristics based on length do not help much. Questions also tend to avoid repeating the stem in the correct option. Instead, the options present parallel alternatives that require discrimination.

The structure is also tightly standardized. Questions are grouped by topic headings, numbered sequentially, and written in a consistent four-option multiple-choice format. The early-grade style uses concise factual recall and routine process knowledge. This confirms that the Ontario dist/coll banks should be regenerated with stricter item-writing rules: short stems, parallel answer construction, plausible distractors, similar option length, and minimal cueing from wording or detail.
